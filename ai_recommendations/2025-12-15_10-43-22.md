### Possible Damages

A voltage sag, even for a short duration like 20 ms, can have significant impacts, especially on sensitive electronic equipment and power electronic converters like the rectifier at BUS 11.

1.  **Damage to Rectifier and DC System (at BUS 11):**
    *   **DIODE_BRIDGE Stress/Failure:** The sudden drop and subsequent recovery of voltage can stress the diodes, potentially leading to immediate failure or reduced lifespan if repeated.
    *   **DC_LINK_CAP Discharge/Damage:** The DC-link capacitor will discharge during the sag to maintain the DC voltage, but if the sag is deep, it might discharge excessively, leading to ripple and potential damage or reduced life. Upon voltage recovery, a sudden inrush current to recharge the capacitor can also cause stress.
    *   **DC_LOAD Disruption/Damage:** The `DC_LOAD` connected to the rectifier will experience a drop in its supply voltage. For critical loads (e.g., control systems, industrial processes), this can lead to:
        *   Maloperation, tripping, or complete shutdown.
        *   Data loss or process interruption.
        *   Damage to sensitive components if the voltage falls below their operational limits or if the recovery is accompanied by transients.
    *   **Control System Malfunction:** The control electronics of the rectifier might misoperate, lose synchronization, or trip protective functions.

2.  **Damage to Other Loads (e.g., LOAD12, LOAD13, LOAD14):**
    *   **Sensitive Electronic Equipment:** Computers, programmable logic controllers (PLCs), variable speed drives (VSDs), and other micro-processor-based equipment are highly susceptible to sags, leading to:
        *   Tripping or resetting, causing production downtime.
        *   Corrupted data or software errors.
        *   Reduced lifespan due to repeated stress.
    *   **Contactors and Relays:** Control circuits might drop out, leading to unexpected shutdown of motors or industrial processes.
    *   **Lighting Systems:** Flickering or momentary outage of lighting.

3.  **Damage to Generators (GEN1, GEN2) and Synchronous Compensators (SYNC_COMP1, SYNC_COMP2, SYNC_COMP3):**
    *   **Loss of Synchronization:** For severe and prolonged sags, generators might lose synchronism with the grid, leading to catastrophic damage if not quickly isolated. (20ms is typically too short for this, but could contribute to instability if other factors are present).
    *   **Mechanical Stress:** Sudden changes in active and reactive power during and after a sag can induce mechanical stress on generator shafts and turbines.
    *   **Excitation System Stress:** The excitation systems of generators and synchronous compensators will work harder to maintain voltage, potentially stressing their components.

4.  **Overall System Impact:**
    *   **System Instability:** A sag on a critical bus can propagate and, in a weak system, potentially trigger further sags or even voltage collapse if not properly mitigated.
    *   **Protection System Misoperation:** Improperly coordinated protection schemes might lead to over-tripping or under-tripping, exacerbating the fault's impact.
    *   **Economic Losses:** Production losses, equipment repair/replacement costs, and revenue loss for utilities.

### IEEE References and Nominal Ranges

Voltage quality and sag characteristics are standardized by various IEEE recommendations and standards.

*   **IEEE 1159-2019: IEEE Recommended Practice for Monitoring Electric Power Quality**
    *   **Definition of Voltage Sag:** Defines a voltage sag (or dip) as a decrease in RMS voltage or current at the power frequency for durations from 0.5 cycles to 1 minute.
    *   **Magnitude:** The residual voltage during a sag is typically between 0.1 pu and 0.9 pu (per unit) of the nominal voltage.
    *   **Duration:** The given fault duration of 20 ms falls well within the typical range for sags. For a 60 Hz system, 20 ms is 1.2 cycles.

*   **IEEE 1346-1998 (R2005): IEEE Recommended Practice for Evaluating Electric Power System Compatibility With Electronic Process Equipment**
    *   Provides guidance on the voltage ride-through capabilities of sensitive industrial equipment during various power quality events, including voltage sags. This standard is crucial for determining if equipment like the rectifier and DC load at BUS 11 can withstand such disturbances.

*   **IEEE 1668-2017: IEEE Recommended Practice for the Application of Uninterruptible Power Supplies in Industrial and Commercial Power Systems**
    *   Offers guidance on selecting and applying UPS systems, which are a primary mitigation strategy for voltage sags affecting critical loads.

*   **Nominal Voltage Ranges (Steady-State):**
    *   For typical power systems, the steady-state voltage is generally expected to be maintained within **±5% of the nominal voltage (i.e., 0.95 pu to 1.05 pu)**. Some standards or utility practices may allow for slightly wider ranges (e.g., ±10% in certain distribution systems).
    *   A voltage sag event implies the voltage has dropped below this nominal operating range, typically below 0.9 pu. The exact nominal voltage for BUS 11 would depend on the system's base voltage.
